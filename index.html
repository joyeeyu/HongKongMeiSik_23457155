- Tea Restaurant -


Tea restaurants, known as "cha chaan teng" in Cantonese, are a beloved part of Hong Kong's culinary culture.
These bustling eateries offer a unique blend of Chinese and Western cuisine, catering to the fast-paced lifestyle of the city. With an extensive menu featuring affordable dishes like crispy toast, Hong Kong-style milk tea, pineapple buns, and instant noodles with various toppings, these restaurants have become iconic. The vibrant atmosphere, lively conversations, and quick service make dining at a cha chaan teng a truly authentic Hong Kong experience.
From locals seeking a taste of nostalgia to tourists exploring the city's food scene, tea restaurants have something for everyone.






This video is a trailer for the "Cha Chaan Teng" TV program.

This program introduces the culture of Hong Kong tea restaurants, including the unique dish names of tea restaurants, changes over time in tea restaurants, and the business methods of tea restaurants, etc.





The tea restaurant has appeared in many movies,
including the famous film "PTU," released in 2003,
and "Love In A Puff," released in 2010.

In fact, it is said that tea restaurants existed as early as 1946.
The restaurant opened by Orchid Garden in Central at that time was the first restaurant to operate under the name of a tea restaurant.




Tea restaurants are renowned for their tasty dishes.

Highlights include the crispy bun with condensed milk and
the pineapple bun with butter, both offering delightful flavors and textures.
The heavenly French toast is a sweet treat, while the classic egg tart satisfies
with its flaky crust and creamy filling.
These dishes provide just a glimpse into the delicious offerings at
tea restaurants.




- Chinese Restaurant -


Hong Kong's Chinese restaurants offer a unique cultural experience, characterized by a vibrant atmosphere, lively conversations, and the pleasant clinking of tea cups that reflect the city's fast-paced nature. Dim sum, a cherished tradition, includes steamed dumplings, crispy spring rolls, and nostalgic dishes that evoke fond memories.
However, as times change and dim sum continues to evolve, acquiring traditional dim sum becomes increasingly challenging. Moreover, the teahouse experience has also transformed. In the past, clerks pushed carts while diners competed to grab their desired items. Nowadays, clerks deliver food directly to the tables.
Here are some noteworthy traditional snacks that exemplify the rich culinary heritage of Hong Kong's Chinese restaurants.


--> The traditional Chinese restaurants of yesteryear are no longer what they used to be.




Black-sesame-rolls

Nostalgic dessert

Black sesame rolls are a specialty dessert in Hong Kong, also known as "film rolls" due to their resemblance to a roll of film.
These rolls were popular in Hong Kong restaurants during the 1970s to the mid-1980s but are now rarely seen.

Steamed Pork Belly & Taro Bun

Nostalgic tea snacks

A few decades ago, Steamed Pork Belly & Taro Bun were a common snack. However, they are almost extinct in Chinese restaurants now, because such snacks are time-consuming and labor-intensive to prepare, and modern people pay attention to healthy eating, so snacks made with ingredients such as lard are becoming less and less popular.

Sweet Egg Twists

Nostalgic dessert

Sweet Egg Twists is made from flour, gluten flour, eggs and lard, rolled into a rope shape, fried until light yellow, and dipped in maltose. In the past, sweet egg twists was so popular in Hong Kong that Hong Kong people used "Sweet Egg Twists" as a derogatory term in Cantonese. Although "Sweet Egg Twists" is sweet, it breaks when touched, so it is used to refer to desperate and timid people.

Sticky Rice Bun

Nostalgic tea snacks

Sticky Rice Bun use siu mai wrappers to wrap seasoned cured glutinous rice. Nowadays, Chinese restaurants rarely have this dim sum, because sticky rice bun are easier to fill up, and customers will not order more dim sum, which will reduce the profit for the restaurant.
